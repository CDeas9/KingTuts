UNEARTHING A COOKBOOK FROM 1226

July 8, 2015

This week King Tut’s is diving into recipes from the Islamic Golden Age. We’ll be co-hosting with Hummus Chick, a cooking workshop this fall for the Frist Center’s exhibit, Ink, Silk and Gold: Islamic Art from the Museum of Fine Arts in Boston, which runs from October 9th, 2015 to January 10, 2016.

Ink Silk and Gold exhibit catalog; Weinstein et al, MFA Publications, 2015

To prepare, we’ve been leafing through a 13th century collection of recipes from one Mohamed Bin Hassan al-Baghdadi. (Some of the recipes are available here in English, pp 75-125.)
Take this short one for “Shawarma:”

Put any type of meat on a large stick and grill it over a low fire, then slice it gradually and serve with onions and ground parsley.

Al-Baghdadi glosses over a number of details, for instance how to spice it, which may have been obvious to the audience at the time, but this still bears a remarkable resemblance to the large, twirling spits of chicken shawarma you’ll find on streets throughout the Middle East. “I remember 80% of these recipes from growing up in Southern Egypt,” says King Tut’s founder, Ragab Rashwan. “We even called them by the same [archaic] names you don’t hear much anymore.”
